Chiefs of Ontario

Evidence-Based Approaches Best Practices

Proven strategies and culturally grounded approaches for supporting First Nations learners through education transitions.

A Chiefs of Ontario initiative, conducted by Mentee

01

Relationship-Based Support

→
Maintain consistent, long-term relationships with learners to build trust and continuity.
→
Ensure continuity of support during staff transitions for smooth handovers and ongoing learner support.
→
Establish clear points of contact at sending and receiving schools or institutions.
→
Schedule regular, predictable check-ins to monitor academic progress, well-being, and to help identify any issues before they become barriers.
→
Communicate regularly with parents, caregivers, and community education staff.
→
Advocate proactively to support learners before, during, and after transitions.
02

Holistic and Wraparound Supports

→
Coordinate academic, wellness, cultural, and practical supports.
→
Clarify referral pathways for mental health, wellness, grief, and addiction services including a list of trusted community-based services and referrals.
→
Provide basic needs supports (e.g., food, emergency assistance) and maintain an up-to-date resource list for emergency funds or crisis support options.
→
Use student-centred approaches that recognize individual strengths and circumstances.
→
Celebrate milestones, key achievements, and multiple pathways to success to build confidence and motivation.
03

Structured Transition Planning

→
Begin transition planning early, in middle or early high school.
→
Conduct orientation activities, campus visits, and pre-entry or transition programs.
→
Provide hands-on support with applications, funding, bursaries, and scholarships.
→
Prepare learners with life-skills (e.g., budgeting, transportation, daily living skills such as time management and organization).
→
Offer incentives and experiential activities that increase engagement and confidence.
04

Culturally Responsive and Indigenous Supports

→
Invest in Indigenous student centres and dedicated Indigenous support staff.
→
Facilitate access to Elders, Knowledge Keepers, and cultural advisors.
→
Provide culturally safe spaces to address racism, trauma, and systemic barriers including clear steps learners can follow to report concerns and access support.
→
Offer cultural programming, ceremony, and land-based learning opportunities.
05

Building Learner Confidence and Responsibility

→
Teach self-advocacy and help-seeking skills early.
→
Incorporate Indigenous frameworks guided by local First Nation teachings (e.g., Medicine Wheel, Grandfather teachings, Haudenosaunee longhouse teachings).
→
Introduce learners gradually to new environments and expectations.
→
Teach practical skills for success such as email use and navigating digital systems.
→
Support learners to set realistic and achievable short- and long-term goals.
06

Proactive Academic and Career Planning

→
Identify learning needs early and provide timely accommodations.
→
Plan courses and pathways aligned with learner interests and goals.
→
Explore various career routes, such as post-secondary, skills and trades, community pathways, and other opportunities aligned with learners’ goals.
→
Encourage learners to actively communicate their needs and aspirations.
07

Clear and Accessible Communication

→
Share information through multiple channels (email, text, phone, social media, in-person).
→
Provide consistent reminders and follow-up rather than one-time messaging to ensure learners remain engaged and supported.
→
Use trusted community networks to share information.
→
Engage families through information sessions and outreach activities.
08

Collaboration and Shared Capacity

→
Build partnerships between First Nations, schools, institutions, and service providers.
→
Participate in regional or provincial networks for frontline education workers.
→
Offer academic and cultural mentorship, along with peer-support opportunities, to foster growth and connectedness.
→
Share resources and tools to reduce duplication and strengthen coordination.

This work is based on feedback received from participants who contributed to the Supporting Education Transitions for First Nations Learners in Ontario project led by Chiefs of Ontario.
